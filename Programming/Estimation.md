# Estimation

## Purpose

An estimate is not a promise.

It is the best prediction that can be made with the information currently available.

The quality of an estimate depends on how well the work is understood and how clearly uncertainty is communicated.

---

## Understand Before Estimating

I avoid estimating work that I do not understand.

Before providing an estimate, I ask as many questions as necessary to clarify the requirements, remove ambiguity, and
understand the project's goals.

Better understanding produces better estimates.

---

## Break Down the Work

I do not estimate large features as a single task.

Instead, I decompose the work into smaller, clearly defined tasks.

Each task receives its own estimate.

Breaking work into smaller pieces improves accuracy, makes progress easier to track, and helps identify risks earlier.

---

## Explain the Estimate

An estimate is more valuable when the client understands what it includes.

I describe each task in detail so expectations are clear.

The estimate should explain both the effort and the scope.

---

## Track Progress Continuously

Estimation does not end after planning.

As individual tasks are completed, I provide continuous visibility into progress.

Clients should always know:

- What has been completed.
- What is currently in progress.
- What remains.
- Whether the original estimate is still valid.

---

## Re-estimate When Necessary

New information changes estimates.

If I discover that a task will take significantly longer than expected, I update the estimate as soon as possible.

An outdated estimate is less useful than an honest revision.

---

## Reduce Estimation Risk

The best way to avoid estimation errors is to reduce uncertainty before committing.

That is why I invest significant effort in asking detailed questions, identifying dependencies, and clarifying
requirements before beginning implementation.

Preparation improves predictability.

---

## Understand the Nature of R&D

Research and development is fundamentally different from routine implementation.

R&D involves:

- Exploration.
- Experimentation.
- Creativity.
- Unknown technical challenges.

Because of this, I expect estimates to evolve as new knowledge is discovered.

The objective is not perfect prediction.

It is transparent communication throughout the process.

---

## Manage Expectations

When estimating work, I prefer realistic commitments over optimistic promises.

I would rather underpromise and overdeliver than create unrealistic expectations that damage trust.

Long-term credibility is more valuable than short-term optimism.

---

## What Success Looks Like

Successful estimation means:

- Requirements are understood before estimating.
- Work is divided into small, measurable tasks.
- Every estimate has a clear scope.
- Progress is continuously communicated.
- Estimates are updated when new information appears.
- Uncertainty is acknowledged rather than hidden.
- Clients understand both the current status and the next steps.
