# Knowledge Principles

## Purpose

LifeOS preserves knowledge that improves my future judgment, decisions, or actions. It does not attempt to comprehensively document external domains. Its value comes from retaining what I have learned, adopted, or need to revisit—not from accumulating the largest possible collection of information.

These principles apply whenever I create, update, review, or remove a document. They protect the repository from becoming an encyclopedic documentation project while keeping it useful as a long-term operating manual.

## What Belongs in LifeOS

Content normally belongs here when it is personal or intentionally adopted, durable, reusable, decision-relevant, and likely to be consulted again. It should help me choose an approach, avoid a recurring mistake, explain a value, or act with greater clarity in a future situation.

The best entries express a lasting principle, a tested workflow, a personal standard, or a concise record of reasoning. They are clearer and more valuable than simply linking to an external source because they explain why the knowledge matters in my own context.

Specialized knowledge can belong here when it captures durable engineering judgment. A focused document about how I evaluate a recurring trade-off is valuable; a broad explanation of every feature in an external system is not.

## What Does Not Belong in LifeOS

LifeOS should not contain exhaustive technology documentation, copied or reorganized vendor documentation, transient notes, raw research dumps, or information kept only because it may be useful someday. Topic coverage should not be driven by completeness, and material that is better maintained by an authoritative external source should remain there.

Links, bookmarks, and short context notes are often enough for external material. Research becomes LifeOS knowledge only after it has been distilled into a durable conclusion, personally adopted practice, or decision that I expect to revisit.

## Before Creating a New Document

Before creating a document, answer these questions:

1. What future decision or action will this improve?
2. Why should this live in LifeOS instead of an external source?
3. Is the knowledge durable?
4. Has it been personally adopted, validated, or made relevant?
5. Can an existing document be updated instead?
6. Would this still be worth preserving in five years?

A new document should not be created when these questions do not have strong answers. Uncertainty is a reason to keep a short external reference or wait for more evidence, not a reason to add a placeholder or broad reference file.

## Prefer Updating Over Adding

Extend an existing document when its subject and purpose are already represented. New files should represent genuinely distinct and durable concepts, not a smaller fragment of a topic that is already covered.

File count is not a measure of progress. Concise documents are preferable to artificial fragmentation, repeated introductions, or separate files created only to make a section appear complete. A document should grow when new material strengthens its central purpose; otherwise, it should be linked externally, merged, or omitted.

## Warning Signs

Pause and review the scope when any of these signs appear:

- Creating topic-completion checklists.
- Feeling responsible for documenting an entire domain.
- Repeatedly creating long reference documents.
- Copying information without extracting a lasting principle.
- Adding documents faster than they are revisited.
- Allowing one area to dominate the identity of LifeOS.
- Treating research volume as progress.

These signals do not mean the underlying topic is unimportant. They indicate that the repository may be storing external reference material instead of personal knowledge.

## Review Outcomes

Future audits may use these outcomes:

- **Keep** — clearly valuable and aligned.
- **Merge** — useful, but does not need its own file.
- **Reduce** — valuable core surrounded by excessive reference material.
- **Remove** — not aligned with the purpose of LifeOS.
- **Uncertain** — leave unchanged until there is enough evidence.

Removal is not a judgment that information is bad. It only means that LifeOS is not the correct place for it. The goal is a smaller, clearer collection that improves future judgment and action.
