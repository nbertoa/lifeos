# Biography

Nicolás Bertoa's professional path began with teaching. From 2009 to 2010, as a teaching assistant at the Faculty of Mathematics, Astronomy and Physics, he helped undergraduate students work through practical programming and mathematics exercises. The role introduced a theme that continued throughout his career: technical understanding becomes more useful when it can be explained and shared.

He entered industry at Intel Corporation as a software engineering intern in 2010 and became a software engineer in 2011. This period established his professional foundation in C++, the Standard Template Library, software engineering practices, and feature and defect work. It also marked the transition from academic exercises to software developed within professional constraints.

In June 2011, Nicolás joined Globant and began a long client engagement with DreamWorks Animation. Over the following nine years, his responsibilities developed from senior C++ development into software design, architecture, and technical-specialist work. The domain also deepened: animation tooling, user interfaces, computer graphics, OpenGL, GLSL, curve editing, performance and memory optimization, production support, and R&D.

That progression was not simply a sequence of titles. It expanded the scale at which he worked. Implementation experience created the context for software-design decisions; sustained work on core systems led to architecture responsibilities; and familiarity with both system behavior and engineering trade-offs supported technical mentoring and reviews. C++, graphics, mathematics, and maintainability remained the connecting foundations.

In 2021, still employed by Globant, Nicolás moved from DreamWorks client work to Sony Interactive Entertainment R&D. The domain shifted from animation software to interactive experiences and VR, using Unreal Engine 4 and 5. Earlier experience in graphics, architecture, optimization, and 3D mathematics transferred naturally to gameplay frameworks, interaction systems, prototyping, and C++/Blueprint architecture.

Unreal Engine is now the central direction of his technical development. He approaches it as both an engine specialization and a place where broader engineering judgment matters: lifecycle and ownership, explicit boundaries, maintainability, performance, debugging, and choosing deliberately between C++ and Blueprints. He is also recorded as an Unreal Authorized Instructor at the Silver level, extending the earlier teaching thread into technical education.

Teaching and mentoring are therefore not separate from Nicolás's engineering work. They appear in university instruction, technical reviews, support for other engineers, and the effort to make difficult decisions understandable. The aim is not to move away from implementation into people management, but to remain hands-on while helping teams make stronger technical decisions.

LifeOS continues that same pattern in written form. It preserves adopted principles, career facts, working methods, and durable lessons so that future decisions do not depend on memory or repeated rediscovery. AI supports this process as a tool for exploration, implementation, review, and learning, while Nicolás retains responsibility for understanding and approving the result.

Across teaching, Intel, DreamWorks client work, Sony Interactive Entertainment R&D client work, and Unreal Engine, the continuity is a steady movement toward technically demanding systems and deeper engineering responsibility. The direction remains continuous learning, practical technical mastery, clear architecture, and knowledge that can be reused and shared.
