# AI Principles

## Purpose

Artificial Intelligence is one of the most powerful productivity tools available to software engineers. Used correctly,
it can accelerate learning, reduce repetitive work, and improve decision making.

These principles define how I use AI to become a better engineer without outsourcing my judgment.

## AI Is a Copilot, Not the Pilot

I use AI to assist my work, not to replace my thinking.

AI helps me explore ideas, explain concepts, generate alternatives, and automate repetitive tasks, but I remain
responsible for every important technical decision.

The final judgment always belongs to me.

## Save Time on Repetitive Work

Time spent on repetitive work is time that cannot be invested in solving meaningful problems.

I use AI to automate boilerplate code, documentation, repetitive refactoring, scripting, formatting, and other
mechanical tasks whenever appropriate.

The goal is to spend more time thinking and less time typing.

## Accelerate Learning

Learning is one of the most valuable uses of AI.

When exploring unfamiliar technologies, I use AI as a teacher that explains concepts, compares approaches, answers
questions, and adapts explanations to my current level of understanding.

The objective is not simply obtaining an answer, but understanding it.

## Explore Alternatives

AI is useful for brainstorming.

When facing a design problem, I ask AI to propose different approaches, explain trade-offs, and challenge my initial
assumptions.

Better decisions often come from evaluating multiple possibilities rather than accepting the first solution.

## Ask for Explanations, Not Just Answers

Whenever AI generates code, I ask it to explain:

- What each important section does.
- Why it chose that approach.
- What alternatives exist.
- What assumptions it made.

Understanding the reasoning behind a solution is often more valuable than the solution itself.

## Match Review to Context

Not every piece of AI-generated code requires the same level of review.

For code that belongs to my area of expertise, such as Unreal Engine or C++, I review the implementation carefully and
validate that it meets my engineering standards.

For small scripts or technologies outside my primary domain, I may rely more heavily on AI's implementation while still
understanding the overall behavior and reasoning.

The level of review should always match the importance of the code, its long-term impact, and my familiarity with the
technology.

## AI Does Not Replace Engineering Responsibility

Regardless of who writes the first draft, I remain responsible for the final result.

Using AI never transfers ownership of software quality, maintainability, security, or correctness.

AI can generate solutions.

Only I can decide whether they belong in the project.

## Anti-Patterns

I avoid using AI as a trial-and-error machine.

One of the worst ways to use AI is to repeatedly ask it to fix code until it appears to work without ever understanding
what changed or why.

That approach creates dependency, hides mistakes, and makes future maintenance significantly harder.

Instead, every iteration should increase my understanding of the problem.

## Continuous Improvement

AI evolves rapidly.

I continuously refine how I use it, adopting new workflows that improve productivity while preserving engineering
judgment, curiosity, and long-term understanding.

My goal is not to become dependent on AI.

My goal is to become a better engineer because of it.
