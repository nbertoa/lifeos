# Decision Making

## Purpose

Every significant decision involves uncertainty.

The quality of a decision depends less on intuition and more on understanding the problem, evaluating alternatives, and
making deliberate trade-offs.

These principles define how I approach decisions in both my personal and professional life.

## Gather Information First

I avoid making important decisions with incomplete information.

Before committing to a solution, I gather the available facts, ask questions, and understand the context as thoroughly
as possible.

Better information leads to better decisions.

## Evaluate Alternatives

I rarely accept the first option that appears.

I compare alternatives, weigh their advantages and disadvantages, and understand the trade-offs involved before making a
commitment.

A deliberate comparison often reveals simpler or safer solutions.

## Prefer Simplicity

When multiple solutions solve the problem, I prefer the simplest one.

Simple solutions are easier to understand, communicate, maintain, and improve.

Complexity should only be introduced when it provides clear and measurable value.

## Minimize Risk

I naturally prefer decisions with a higher probability of success over decisions with higher potential rewards but
significantly greater uncertainty.

Predictable progress is usually more valuable than speculative success.

Reducing unnecessary risk allows consistent long-term results.

## Reduce Uncertainty Before Committing

Uncertainty is not a reason to stop.

It is a signal that more information is needed.

I prefer asking more questions, validating assumptions, or building small experiments before committing to large
decisions.

Confidence should come from understanding, not optimism.

## Accept Trade-offs

Every decision involves compromise.

There is rarely a perfect solution.

The objective is to choose the option that best balances quality, time, cost, complexity, and long-term value.

Understanding trade-offs is more important than chasing perfection.

## Commit Once Decided

Once I have enough information and have selected a direction, I commit to it.

Constantly revisiting decisions without new information wastes time and creates unnecessary uncertainty.

Good decisions require both analysis and commitment.

## Take Ownership When Things Go Wrong

Mistakes happen.

The first step is identifying who is responsible for resolving the problem.

If I am responsible, I communicate the issue early, make the situation visible, take ownership, and immediately begin
working on a solution.

Accountability builds trust.

## Learn From Every Decision

Every decision provides feedback.

Successful decisions reinforce good judgment.

Poor decisions reveal assumptions that need to be challenged.

The goal is not to avoid mistakes completely, but to continuously improve the quality of future decisions.

## What Success Looks Like

A good decision is one that:

- Is based on sufficient information.
- Considers multiple alternatives.
- Balances trade-offs consciously.
- Prefers simplicity over unnecessary complexity.
- Minimizes unnecessary risk.
- Is communicated clearly.
- Is owned responsibly.
- Improves future decision making through reflection.
